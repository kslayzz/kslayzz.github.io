# Understanding BEPS 2.0 and Its Impact on Indian Multinationals

The OECD's Base Erosion and Profit Shifting (BEPS) 2.0 framework — comprising Pillar One and Pillar Two — represents the most significant overhaul of international tax rules in a generation. For Indian multinationals with global operations, the implications are both immediate and far-reaching.

## What Is BEPS 2.0?

BEPS 2.0 is the OECD/G20 Inclusive Framework's two-pillar solution to the tax challenges arising from the digitalisation of the global economy. Over 140 countries have agreed to its broad architecture, with implementation now underway across key jurisdictions.

**Pillar One** reallocates taxing rights over the profits of the largest multinationals (revenue over €20 billion, profit margin above 10%) to market jurisdictions — that is, where customers are located — regardless of physical presence. A portion of "residual profits" (Amount A) will be taxable where value is consumed, not just created.

**Pillar Two** introduces a global minimum tax of **15%** on the profits of multinational groups with consolidated revenue exceeding **€750 million**. The key mechanism is the Income Inclusion Rule (IIR), which allows a parent company's home jurisdiction to "top up" tax if a subsidiary is taxed below 15% anywhere in the world.

## Why It Matters for Indian Companies

India is an active participant in the Inclusive Framework and has committed to implementing Pillar Two. Several dimensions are directly relevant to Indian multinationals:

### 1. Subsidiaries in Low-Tax Jurisdictions

Indian groups that have set up holding companies, IP holding entities, or financing subsidiaries in jurisdictions like Mauritius, Singapore, Netherlands, or UAE — often for legitimate commercial reasons — now face the prospect of top-up taxes being levied in India if the effective tax rate (ETR) in those jurisdictions falls below 15%.

The **Qualified Domestic Minimum Top-up Tax (QDMTT)** is a mechanism countries can adopt to collect this top-up themselves before India (or another parent jurisdiction) does. Several jurisdictions that Indian companies use have already enacted QDMTTs.

### 2. The GloBE Information Return

Pillar Two compliance involves filing a new global information return — the **GloBE Information Return (GIR)** — in each jurisdiction where the group has entities. This return discloses country-by-country effective tax rates, covered taxes, and qualifying income. The data burden is substantial, particularly for groups without a centralised tax function.

### 3. Impact on Existing Tax Incentives

India offers various tax incentives — SEZ deductions, Patent Box regimes for IP, and production-linked incentives in certain sectors. Under the GloBE rules, some of these incentives may reduce the ETR below 15%, triggering top-up obligations. However, **Qualified Refundable Tax Credits (QRTCs)** are treated differently and may partially insulate certain incentive regimes.

### 4. Outbound Investment Structuring

Indian companies that have invested abroad for operational, treasury, or holding purposes will need to re-examine their structures. Entities that were tax-efficient under pre-BEPS rules may now generate top-up tax liabilities. This requires a fresh look at:

- Substance in offshore entities (do they meet the **Substance-Based Income Exclusion** carve-out?)
- ETR calculations at an entity and jurisdictional level
- Whether to restructure or absorb the top-up cost

## Key Concepts Every CFO Should Understand

| Term | What It Means |
|------|---------------|
| GloBE Rules | Global Anti-Base Erosion rules — the Pillar Two framework |
| ETR | Effective Tax Rate — calculated as covered taxes divided by GloBE income |
| IIR | Income Inclusion Rule — parent charges top-up on low-taxed subsidiary |
| UTPR | Undertaxed Profits Rule — backstop rule when IIR is not applied |
| QDMTT | Qualified Domestic Minimum Top-up Tax — local top-up collected locally |
| SBIE | Substance-Based Income Exclusion — carve-out for payroll and tangible assets |

## What Indian Groups Should Do Now

### Step 1: Scope Assessment
Identify whether your group meets the €750 million revenue threshold. Groups approaching this threshold should track it annually — acquisitions can trigger applicability unexpectedly.

### Step 2: ETR Modelling
Map every jurisdiction where the group has entities. Calculate the current ETR for each using GloBE methodology (which differs from statutory rates — deferred tax adjustments, excluded dividends, and covered taxes are key inputs).

### Step 3: Identify Exposed Entities
Flag jurisdictions where ETR falls below 15%, or where it is close to 15% and could dip below due to dividend distributions, timing differences, or changed business activity.

### Step 4: Review Incentive Structures
Work with your tax advisors to assess which incentive regimes qualify as QRTCs and which reduce covered taxes in a way that creates GloBE exposure.

### Step 5: Data and Systems Readiness
GloBE compliance requires data at a granular level — entity-by-entity, jurisdiction-by-jurisdiction. Groups that rely on decentralised accounting or manual consolidation processes will need to invest in systems and processes before their first filing obligation arises.

## India's Legislative Position

India incorporated the Pillar Two framework into the Union Budget 2024 speech, signalling intent to legislate. The Income Tax Act is expected to be amended to include IIR and QDMTT provisions. Detailed rules, including transitional safe harbours, are anticipated in upcoming Finance Acts.

The **transitional Country-by-Country Reporting (CbCR) safe harbour** — which simplifies the GloBE calculation for three years using existing CbCR data — provides breathing room for groups to build compliance infrastructure.

## Conclusion

BEPS 2.0 is not a distant regulatory development — it is already live in the EU, UK, South Korea, Japan, and several other key jurisdictions. Indian multinationals with operations in these geographies will encounter Pillar Two in their foreign subsidiaries' filings, even before India enacts domestic legislation.

The time to act is now: model your exposure, understand your data gaps, and review structures before top-up taxes crystallise into actual cash costs.
